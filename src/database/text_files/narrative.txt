### Roleplay Basics
In narrative gameplay players roleplay as their characters, using their character's goals, abilities, and knowledge to inform their decisions.
&nbsp;

Narrative play in Shattered Spirits is typically fast-paced and requires few dice rolls; a character's Archetype, Talents, and Spiritcraft provide guidance on what a character is capable of through Skills, as well as  specific abilities that can be used to enhance their results.

&nbsp;

### Skill Challenges
In many situations, dice are not needed to resolve a situation in Shattered Spirits. If a character has the _Strong_ _Archetype_, they should be able to accomplish many strength-based tasks without consulting the dice. As a general rule of thumb, if there is no time constraint, minimal consequences to failure, and the character can simply retry something as-needed then a character with the appropriate Skills should simply succeed in the task. A character's Skills and magic can accomplish many things without the need to create a skill challenge, and a character's abilities should be respected.

&nbsp;

However, when the stakes are high or the outcome is unclear characters must make a Skill Challenge using their Stakes Sheet.

&nbsp;

First, a character should describe how they're attempting to solve a problem. Based on that, the GM will determine two things: whether the roll is _Cautious_ or _Bold_ as well as what the _Stakes_ are, shown below.

&nbsp;

Stakes are the good or bad things that can happen during a skill challenge and, based on how you roll, you can add _Augments_ and remove _Dangers_. _Augments_ tend to allow your character to accomplish more and increase how successful something is, while _Dangers_ are hazards and pitfalls that can occur along the way. The GM will typically decide at least one automatic stake that cannot be removed, whether that be an _Augment_ or _Danger_. In addition, your GM will list a few potential _Dangers_, as well as what _Augments_ may be relevant.
&nbsp;

Characters will then determine how many dice they roll. Players automatically roll 2 dice, but may gain or lose die ones based on a variety of factors:
* If a character has appropriate Equipment for the situation, they gain an additional die.
* If a character has an appropriate Skill or their magic or spirit would be helpful, they gain an additional die, or two if it is an exceptionally good fit. This may trigger multiple times.
* If a character has appropriate Conditions they may gain bonus die from those.
* If a character has done work to prepare for a situation or has set up some other advantage, they may gain a die.
* A character may take 1 _Stress_ to gain an additional die. In some circumstances the GM may decide that the character must take 1 _Fatigue_ instead, typically for checks that occur over a long timespan such as Travel.
* A single ally may assist in the roll, providing a bonus die if appropriate.
* If a character has a Condition that would hinder their ability to succeed, they may lose a die.
* If a character's _Health Bar_ is full, they roll one less die.

&nbsp;

At this point, a character may take 1 _Stress_ to swap from Bold to Cautious, or vice versa. Additionally, if characters have abilities that allow them to modify the Stakes sheet, they may use them here.

&nbsp;

After determining the character's die pool, characters roll their d6s. A result of 1-2 is a &#8863;, a result of 3-4 is a &#9634;, and a result of 5-6 is a &#8862;. If acting _Cautiously_, characters reroll &#9634; and &#8862;, while if they are rolling _Boldly_ they reroll &#8863; and &#9634;. If a character is rolling _Skillfully_ (usually from some ability), they may reroll dice of their choice. If a character does not wish to reroll, they may take 1 _Stress_ to use their initial result.

&nbsp;

After completing rolls, characters assign their available dice to Augments and Dangers. The GM then narrates the scene, applies appropriate consequences and conditions, and the scene continues. Sometimes, a single _Skill Challenge_ will be enough to finish a situation but in many cases characters will take turns accomplishing Skill Challenges until they've accomplished their goal or failed trying.

&nbsp;
### Using Spiritcraft
A character's Spirit Talents will outline the general guidelines of how a character's spiritcraft may be used. For checks that fit perfectly within these bounds characters may freely use their spiritcraft. A character may temporarily push beyond the limits of their spiritcraft (either in terms of power or duration, such as using their craft for an entire day) but must mark either _Fatigue_ or _Stress_ for their Spirit when they do so, depending on how out of the bounds of their magic this ability is.

&nbsp;
### Group Checks
In many cases multiple party members may wish to attempt a Skill Challenges, or it may make narrative sense for the entire party to participate. In these cases, have each character attempt their own individual components of the Skill Challenges, but always include the following stake: <u>Aid (Advantage)</u>: Any dice placed on this Augment can be given to an ally, who rolls once and adds the result of those die to their result.

&nbsp;
### Abbreviated Skill Challenge
In general, characters should either be able to automatically do something, be unable to do something, or begin a Skill Challenge. However, in some situations, there might be a question of whether or not a character can succeed, but it isn't worth consulting an entire stakes sheet for the scenario. In such cases, a GM should determine how many &#8862; are needed to succeed, as well as a single penalty for failure. The character will create their dice pool as normal then roll, counting up the number of &#8862; they achieved. Characters do not reroll due to attempting something _Cautiously_, _Boldly_, or _Skillfully_ in an Abbreviated Skill Challenge. If a character achieves enough &#8862; they succeed; otherwise, they fail.

&nbsp;
When a skill check is called for in combat, an Abbreviated Skill Challenge is always attempted. Typically, there is no penalty for failing beyond the resources spent to attempt this.

&nbsp;
### Knowledge and Noticing
Characters in Shattered Spirits should never roll to know or notice something. Knowledge is covered by a character's Skills, and should largely use that as a reference. If a character is a smith, they should automatically know anything that a smith might without any rolls. Different Skills may reveal different information, and sometimes Skills won't be relevant, but knowledge should be freely shared without rolls.
&nbsp;
For noticing, finding, or other perception-based effects, if a character has a relevant Skill they should receive that information for free, without any roll. If characters are looking for a locket in a desk and have Metalsense, they should find it quickly and easily. Otherwise, ask players how they're searching an area, what they're interacting with, and how long they're willing to spend there and use that information to determine if anything is found.  
&nbsp;
If a Skill Challenge, the _Observation_ stake can be used for uncovering hidden information, and is a great way to handle characters attempting to find hidden information with a limited amount of time.
&nbsp;
### Narrative Resources

In narrative play characters must manage their Health and Spirit Bars.
&nbsp;
A character's Health Bar represents their general wellbeing and tracks several potential conditions: Stress, Fatigue, and Wounds, as well as more specialized variants of those.
&nbsp;

* Stress represents a character's mental state. Stress is easy to gain and cannot typically be cleared simply through rest; it requires the assistance of allies and resources to clear. Stress on its own has no impact, but can rapidly fill a character's Health Bar.
* Fatigue is general physical ills caused from long hikes, combat, and the labors of adventuring. Fatigue can be cleared with sufficient rest. In combat, characters lose 1 _Guard_ for each Fatigue at the start of the Round.
    * Hunger and Thirst are variants of Fatigue caused by not having enough to eat or drink. Rather than clearing with rest, these are instead cleared when an extra portion of food or drink are consumed, although only one of each may be cleared per day.
* Wounds are the most serious of injuries, and represent distinct physical injuries. The severity of a wound is measured in the number of sections it takes up, with more sections corresponding to a worse wound. Each wound is bespoke and carries with it a Condition Clock. When filled the Wound has healed significantly, which lessens the intensity of the Wound and typically replaces part with Fatigue. They must clear the new Wound as normal. Wounds may make certain skill challenges more difficult and inflict _Afflictions_ on the character at the start of each round of combat.
&nbsp;

Common effects that fills the Health Bar are as follows:
* Characters can take Stress to push themselves and gain additional effects on skill checks.
* Tense or dangerous situations can result in characters gaining _Stress_.
* Characters automatically gain 1 Fatigue when participating in combat, and Fatigue is a common stake for traveling that represents the difficulties of it.
* If a character is _Downed_ in combat, they roll for _Wounds_ (see the combat rules).
* If a character does not have food or water for a day they gain Hunger or Thirst, as appropriate.
&nbsp;

A character's Spirit has a Health Bar as well, representing their reserves of magic as well as the physical health. Spirits typically cannot receive medical attention, and use the <u>Channel</u> _Camp Action_ to clear Wounds or <u>Pamper</u> to clear Stress.
&nbsp;

Common things that fill the spirit's Health Bar are as follows:
* Participating in combat always causes 1 Fatigue.
* Using spirit abilities or pushing your limits (using an ability for an entire travel day, for example) may cause your Spirit to gain _Stress_ or _Fatigue_.
* If a Spirit is manifested for the majority of a day of travel, they gain _Fatigue_.
* _Fatigue_ for your Spirit is a common stake for challenges.
* If a spirit assists you in in a challenge,  split _Injury_ Dangers between the two of you.
* If your spirit falls in combat they take _Wounds_, rolling as normal.
&nbsp;

If a spirit's Health Bar is ever filled they cannot use magic and their spirit is incapable of helping in tasks or combat, although they may still be manifested. A character may inflict a single segment Wound on their spirit to be able to use their magic for a single Scene.
&nbsp;

If a spirit's Health Bar is full and is all Wounds the spirit is put into critical condition and will die if pushed any further. If a spirit dies its character loses all spiritcraft. Losing a spirit causes significant psychological harm and tends to mark the end of a spiritbound's career as an adventurer.

&nbsp;
If a character gains a condition and the appropriate bar is full, instead upgrade the lowest rank effect in that bar to the next tier (Stress->Fatigue->Wound). If upgrading Stress or Fatigue, also inflict a Condition on the character with 2-4 segments. For example, if a character was forced to upgrade a Hunger to a Wound, they might gain the Condition 'Starving', and be unable to clear Hunger until that clock was full. If a Health Bar would be more than filled by an effect instead fill it as much as possible, try to upgrade any remaining effects, then use a Clock for any remainder.
&nbsp;
Additionally, how full a character's _Health Bar_ is has an impact on their abilities. If a character's _Health Bar_ is full but not all _Wounds_ they roll one less die on every roll. If a character's _Health Bar_ is full and all _Wounds_ they are instead incapable of further adventure; pushing themselves beyond this point could mean certain death. Taking an additional _Wound_ will result in the character's death, and taking a _Fatigue_ or _Stress_ will carry lasting consequences.
&nbsp;
### Gameplay Examples
#### The Flooded Tunnel
The party, through a series of mistakes, has been caught in an Underworld trap, and find themselves in a hallway that is rapidly flooding, where the only viable path of escape is through a solid metal door. John wants to use his spiritcraft to weaken the door, then pry it open with his anvilcraft enhanced sword. The GM thinks this sounds like a reasonable way out, and says that John will be rolling _Boldly_.
&nbsp;
With that established, the GM begins to lay out stakes; first off, as an automatic stake, the GM decides that John will make some progress and weaken the door, providing an _Advantage_ stake for the next attempt to break this down in case of failure. The GM then says that they'll set up a four-segment clock to open the door, and automatically add one segment for this attempt. Finally, the entire party will take a _Stress_ due to being in the trap.
&nbsp;
The GM decides this is dangerous and sets out a large number of Dangers; _Mayhem_, _Indiscretion_, and _Expense_ _Dangers_ for the potential to damage the ruins, create an immense amount of noise, and risk of breaking the sword. He additionally lays out a _Stress_ for Kit, John's Spirit, as this is a fairly intense use of spiritcraft and pushes the limits of what John can accomplish. For Augments, the GM says that _Speed_ will let this happen faster, and _Duration_ will avoid damaging the door in such a way that it might be closed and block the passage after everyone is through. Finally, the GM says each rank of _Efficacy_ will fill another segment on the Clock to open the door. John agrees with the stakes and wants to get through this tunnel as quickly as possible, so keeps the roll _Bold_.
&nbsp;
John starts with two die, as normal for Skill Challenges. The GM decides that, due to the door being made of metal, anvilcraft is certainly helpful here, as is John's background as a Smith, granting three die (one for Anvilcraft, two for Smith). Between anvilcraft and the sword John the GM also agrees that John has sufficient Equipment to help here, granting another die. Finally, Marlon decides to take a _Stress_ and help, using his tidecraft to give John a small water-free area to work, for a final total of seven die. John rolls, coming up with three &#8863;, two &#9634;, and two &#8862;. As he is rolling _Boldly_, he rerolls everything except the &#8862;, this time coming up with one &#9634; and six &#8862;, for a final total of six &#8862;. With this, John purchases _Efficacy_ three times, _Speed_, and _Duration_. The last &#8862; cannot be spent on anything and is lost.
&nbsp;
John rips open the door before the party is carried through by a powerful current, damaging some support pillars by the trap; due to the _Mayhem_ stake, exiting through this part of the ruins is next to impossible now. The door also slammed into the wall hard from this effort, echoing loudly throughout the ruins. The party was submerged much of the way, and all suffer _Stress_ from the experience, while Kit additionally suffers _Stress_ from the intense spiritcraft. The party has escaped the trap, but must now contend with the new dangers caused by it. Fortunately, due to the _Duration_ _Augment_, John was able to bend the door back before being fully ripped away, ensuring the trap won't cause them any more issues.
&nbsp;
Normally, the _Expense_ stake would break John's sword, but due to his _Novice Anvilcraft_, he can roll a d6 to prevent that. John fails the roll, but decides to have Kit take a _Fatigue to not damage his sword.
&nbsp;
#### Finding the Missing Child
Winona is attempting to track down information about somebody in Phoenix Village that went missing. She's had a bit of a rough time traveling and has a full _Health Bar_, and doesn't want to risk further incidents so, rather than attempting to find a trail and track it, decides to try to see what she can learn from the other hunters in the village, but doesn't want to try to press anybody too hard. The GM says that's a sound way to gather information, and says that Winona will be rolling _Cautiously_.
&nbsp;
The GM decides that, regardless of how Winona rolls, she'll at least learn some rumors of dangerous beasts in the area. For _Dangers_, the GM only puts out  _Expense_ and _Menace_, to represent needing to trade for or bribe folks for good information, or offending a townsfolk while prying for information. As far as _Augments_, the GM states that _Efficacy_ will provide more specific information and can be played any number of times, and _Observation_ will grant some useful information about the area not related to the missing child.
&nbsp;
Unfortunately, Winona lacks any specific talents for carousing but, as she's focusing her attempts on hunters and outdoorsmen, her _Hunter_ and _Ranger_ Skills would apply, granting two die. Additionally, as the townsfolk are actively interested in helping find the child, the GM grants an extra die. Unfortunately, that's all Winona has and, with her full _Health Bar_ deducting a die, that brings her to only two extra die for 4 total.
&nbsp;
For her initial die, Winona rolls two &#8863;, one &#9634; and one &#8862;. After rerolling the &#9634; and &#8862; she comes up with a final total of two &#8863; and two &#8862;. With this, she's able to buy down both _Dangers_ as well as two ranks of _Efficacy_. With that, Winona find Arvid, a hunter in the area and immediately hits it off. He's also been looking for the lost child, and has a theory about a wind-spirit he's seen in the area that hovers around the town. It's small enough to sneak into town, and would explain the complete lack of a trail well. He has no ideas how to track or find it, but thinks that it seemed to linger near children in the village, which might provide some sort of way to lure the creature.
