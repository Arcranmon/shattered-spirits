### Traveling in the Shattered World
The Shattered World is a beautiful, wild, dangerous place, and travelers leaving the safety of their town or village must be well-prepared. Besides the obvious dangers of hunger, thirst, weather, and dangerous beasts, the Shattered World carries with it other, greater dangers, like impossible weather, territorial spirits and, of course, areas still ravaged by the Shattering itself.
&nbsp; 

### The Phases of a Journey
Play in Shattered Spirits is structured around the phases of a Journey. Each Journey represents a trip away from home, and is where the bulk of gameplay comes in.

&nbsp;

Before beginning a Journey, characters first have to find why they're going on a Journey! Common motivations are to find a rare resource or medicine, find a piece of lost knowledge, or to beg a boon from a distant spirit. Early on these motivations will likely be more concrete but, as the players explore the world and make bonds with NPCs, they may come up with journeys that they wish to attempt.

&nbsp; 

Once a goal and destination are decided, characters will then break their Journey down into Legs, which represent the primary stops in the Journey. Some shorter Journeys may only have a single Leg (journey origin to the final destination), but longer Journeys will frequently be comprised of multiple Legs. Typically, each stop will be a place the party can rest, recuperate, and refresh themselves before the next destination, as well as delineating significant differences in terrain. 

&nbsp; 

Once players have established their Legs, the GM will assign each a travel distance, which is tracked using a Clock, and create the appropriate Journey Decks. From there, players will play through travel days, making progress to their destination, finding discoveries, and dealing with every obstacle that comes in their way. At the start of each day, the GM will determine the day's weather and any major events, such as roaming spirits or other travelers.

&nbsp;

From there, the GM will lay out the days' Stakes, and the players will make their Travel Roll, play through the **Travel Deck** (which is built from the **Menace Deck**, **Event Deck**, and **Discovery Deck**), then make camp for the night, rest, and prepare for the next day. Characters continue to make Travel Rolls until they have accumulated enough travel progress to fill the Clock, which indicates that they'll reach their goal on that day of travel. From there, the game plays as normal, with players overcoming the obstacles required to accomplish their goal or simply resting in a town to prepare for the next phase of travel. Once players reach their final goal and accomplish (or fail to accomplish) their task, the process begins again with the return journey to make it back home.
&nbsp;
The _Menace Deck_ represents issues the party can run into while traveling; some severe, and some quite mundane. Typically, for a relatively safe area, there should be one card per two characters, but as the area becomes more dangerous this increases. In addition to the dangers caused by the region, characters will add cards to the _Menace Deck_ in some situations:
* If a character has at least half their _Health Bar_ filled, add a _Minor Mishap_ to the _Menace Deck_. When drawn, it will always include them.
* If a character has at least half their _Health Bar_ filled with _Fatigue_ or _Wounds_, add a _Mishap_ to the _Menace Deck_. When drawn, it will always include them.

&nbsp;

### The Travel Roll
Each day when the party attempts to make progress to their destination they should make a Travel Roll, which is a specialized Skill Challenge. In general, characters don't get bonus dice for a travel roll except for abilities that specifically grant them. Most bonus dice will be granted by either knowledge of the route, such as experience or a detailed map, or a Career that is granting a situational bonus.

&nbsp;

Like any Skill Challenge, the GM will first lay out the stakes, many of which are standardized for the Travel Roll, and players must decide if they are being **Bold** or **Cautious** in their travel. In almost every case, there will be one Automatic Stake, **Speed**, which grants a single segment on the progress clock_. In general, all characters make their Travel roll separately, although some Stakes may benefit the whole party or may be offset by a single character. In these cases, provide an additional stakes sheet that only has the **Dangers** and **Augments** for the whole party to simplify tracking whether those have been dealt with.

&nbsp;

While traveling, the following are common Stakes that the GM might use:

&nbsp;
**Dangers:**
* **Affliction:** In a dangerous areas with odd weather or terrain **Fatigue** or other afflictions might be a danger, although appropriate equipment may remove those dangers automatically.
* **Delay:** In areas where navigation is difficult this Danger removes a segment from the Clock for the current Leg, after adding any **Speed** from the current one. A single character can offset this **Danger** for the whole party.
* **Expense:** In areas of extreme heat, characters must mark off a day of water of gain **Thirst**. Other, more exotic climates might require a different resource to navigate, such as antidotes in a toxic swamp.
* **Affliction (_Fatigue_):** Generally, there will always be a **Fatigue** for a character and their spirit as separate stakes, but particularly rough terrain may cause additional instances of this stake.
* **Indiscretion:** This stake is usually present, and represents the party leaving a trail. Each party member needs to offset this individually.
* **Menace:** When a card from the _Menace Deck_ is drawn, this _Danger_ may be removed to reveal the card. This represents characters scouting out dangers and finding risks before they happen. This may allow the party to completely avoid a hazard but, more often, it will provide significant advantage when dealing with the problem as the party notices it advance.

&nbsp;

**Augments:**
* **Advantage:** You find a good site to camp for the night; you may either remove a negative modifier on your camp tonight (if applicable) or add _Fortified_, _Hidden_, _Defensible_, or _Comfortable_.
* **Efficacy:** Choose a single Job to work on during this travel. Any die placed on this Job increase the effect. Many Careers provide additional jobs, but common ones are below:
    * **Aid:** You may give any die spent on this to other characters, who may immediately roll them and spend them on **Dangers**/**Augments** as appropriate.
    * **Chart:** Mark one segment on the Route Mastery Clock for this route. Only one character may choose this option per day, and only one segment may be placed on it.
    * **Forage:** Roll 2d6 and consult the **Forage Chart**, gaining that result or any lower than it on the table. For each additional die placed on this roll gain a +1 to this roll.
    * **Hunt:** As **Forage**, but instead roll on the **Hunting Chart**. Characters may only select this option if they have some sort of ranged attack suitable for hunting (typically an Attack that is at least Range 2).
* **Observation:** When a _Discovery_ is drawn from the _Travel Deck_, characters may use this _Augment_ to explore it.

&nbsp;

The GM will create the travel deck for the day, and the players will play out the events of that day of travel. While travel plays out characters may freely spend their die as needed, either to accomplish additional deeds or to aid in tasks while traveling.

&nbsp;

When traveling, the total progress marked is the lowest progress that any party member achieved. This means that all characters must play the **Speed** Advantage the same number of times; be flexible with allowing players to rearrange how they spent their dice to make sure nobody gets left behind.

&nbsp;
The standards for Equipment providing a bonus are significantly higher during travel. Most relevant Equipment will specify its bonuses, such as automatically removing a Danger related to a specific effect.
&nbsp;
Generally, characters are assumed to have their spirit Manifested as needed while they travel, and rely on their spirit for minor assistance throughout the travel day, hence the Fatigue stake for them. However, a character may choose to keep their spirit unmanifested for travel and automatically remove any stakes that affect their spirit; if they do, they must minimize spiritcraft used.

&nbsp;
### Tracking Legs
It's likely that there will be travel legs that players do multiple times over the course of their adventuring career. As they travel, characters should track their _Mastery_ and _Affinity_ for routes, as well as any discoveries they have made.
&nbsp;
A Leg's _Mastery_ represents how well characters know it and is represented by a clock. When characters travel and use the <u>Chart</u> job they make progress on a Leg's _Mastery_, and may also gain _Mastery_ from other sources such as maps or gathering information. When the clock is filled the characters gain +1 die for all future travel on that Leg.
&nbsp;
A Leg's _Affinity_ represents how well-liked the party is by the local spirits of the _Leg_. Typically, this will be a three to four segment Clock that is filled when the party makes offerings or otherwise assists the local spirits. Whenever they are on that _Leg_ characters may clear segments on the clock for +1 die on a _Travel Roll_ or _Skill Challenge_ as the local spirits provide some small assistance.
&nbsp;
Discoveries should be tracked so characters always know where they can find a friendly spirit, have a safe place to camp, or can otherwise make a useful detour along their journey.
### Making Camp
After a long day of travel, the party will typically make camp. When they do, the party has a chance to decompress, gather themselves, and rest. When resting, each party member can do a single **Camp Action**, and then makes their **Rest Roll**.

&nbsp;

**Camp Actions** are all small things that need to be done around the camp, or can help a character properly recover. Each character can normally do one per Camp Phase. In general, basic tasks such as breaking camp, keeping watch, making a fire, and setting up tents are done without a character using their Camp Action. Common Camp Actions are as follows:
* **Channel:** You may only take this action if you have considerable _Source_ for your Spirit. Your spirit rolls an additional 2 die during its _Rest Roll_, and may automatically play _Efficacy_ once.
* **Connect:** Choose another character to spend time talking and bonding with. The two of you roleplay a quick scene; afterwards, the two of you gain a _Bond_ and both roll a die. On a 4+, clear a _Stress_.
* **Cook:** Much of the food brought on journeys doesn't requires cooking, but game caught on the trail or other similar foods must be cooked to be safely eaten. This action requires Cooking Tools and a fire.
* **Forage/Hunt:** As the options during Travel, but take a -1 _Penalty_.
* **Mend:** Choose a piece of Equipment that is missing _Durability_ but not _Destroyed_. If you have the appropriate tools you may expend _Supplies_ to restore 1 _Durability_.
* **Pamper:** You spend time with your spirit and taking care of it, whether that be playing with it, styling it, or something similar. Afterwards, you or your spirit clears a _Stress_.
* **Patch:** Expend _Medicine_ or _Supplies_. You or another character may immediately mark one segment on a Wound Clock. A character may only benefit from this once per Camp.
* **Rest:** You and your Spirit gain an additional die on your _Rest Roll_.
* **Work:** Camps will have modifiers, some of which may make it more difficult to camp. Characters may this _Camp Action_ to attempt to remove a harmful modifier, or add a positive modifier such as _Fortified_ or _Hidden_. Oftentimes this will require _Supplies_ or a specialized skillset to accomplish.
&nbsp;
Additionally, without using your _Camp Action_, you may _Cut Loose_, representing potentially destructive behavior that relieves _Stress_. Several items allow this, but the most common way to do this is using _Supplies_.

&nbsp;

After finishing their Camp Action, characters will mark off _Supplies_ to avoid gaining _Hungry_ and additional _Supplies_ to avoid gaining _Thirsty_. Some effects allow characters to consume additional _Supplies_ for some effect, but a character may only consume a single extra portion. Characters and spirits then make a **Rest Roll** to see how well they're able to recover. The **Rest Roll** typically has an automatic Stake of a single **Efficacy**, and often includes the following:

&nbsp;
**Dangers**
* **Affliction (Crash):** You sleep far too heavily or are otherwise unaware, leaving you unresponsive to any dangers for the night.

&nbsp;
**Augments**
* **Efficacy:** Remove a **Fatigue** from yourself or mark one segment on a Wound Clock. Spirits may instead choose to restore a _Durability_ to any _Equipment_ granted to them by their _Traits_.

&nbsp;
If characters are unable to get a quality rest either due to not finding a sufficiently safe place to camp or missing essential equipment such as bedrolls or tents they take a 1-2 die penalty on their roll. If conditions are sufficiently dire additional Dangers may be added. Characters always roll their **Rest Roll** **Cautiously**, unless specified otherwise.
&nbsp;
Additionally, depending on the environment, spirits may gain or lose die. When they rest in an area aligned with their element or with a large amount of their element they can use to heal, they gain a die on their _Rest Roll_. If the area is strongly opposed to their element, they instead lose a die. Finally, if they are in a _Place of Power_, they may roll _Boldly_.

### Travel Pace
All the above rules assume a normal travel pace. Characters may make the following changes to their Travel/Rest for the following effects:
* **Extended Stay:** Skip the _Travel Roll_ today; you make no progress and remain in your camp. You may make your _Rest Roll_ _Boldly_, automatically benefit from the _Rest_ _Camp Action_ and may do two additional _Camp Actions_.
* **Explore:** Lose the automatic **Speed** stake, but roll +1 die on your **Travel Roll**.
* **Forced March:** You must be traveling _Boldly_ to choose this. Gain an additional automatic **Speed** stake. Take an additional **Fatigue**, and roll -1 die on your **Travel Roll**.
* **Through the Night:** As **Forced March** but, additionally, add an additional _Speed_ stake. Do not make camp (causing an automatic _Fatigue_ from not sleeping), but you may still eat and drink to avoid _Hungry_/_Thirst_.
* **Double Shift:** You may take an additional _Camp Action_. You may not take the _Rest_ Camp Action tonight. Additionally, you lose the automatic _Efficacy_ stake when resting.

### Gameplay Example
John, Margaret, Winona and Erika are all beginning their journey from Serpent Village to Library, a long trip across the Greenvale. To start, the GM sets the overall distance for the Journey, and, after looking at the map, decides an 8 segment _Clock_ is appropriate. It's a long trip, and will likely take several days for the party to get there but the terrain is largely the same and, as such, the trip doesn't need to be split up into multiple Lgs.
&nbsp;
With that established, the GM gets the remainder of his resources together: a series of mishaps and dangers to fill the _Menace Deck_, interesting discoveries for the _Discovery Deck_, and additional events such as weather or other travelers to fill the _Event Deck_. Additionally, they prepare the _Hunting_ and _Forage_ Charts for the _Greenvale_, and informs the party that, by default, the camp they find will be _Wooded_ and _Waterside_; the Greenvale is full of lush plant life and finding water is never difficult. With that preparation done, the GM and players begin the journey! To start, the GM describes the weather and climate of the first day; clear skies as far as the eye can see and mild weather.
&nbsp;
To start, the GM lets the players know on the first day of travel they'll get an additional bonus die due to being very familiar with the area around Serpent Village, and due to the relative safety of the area. The party isn't overly concerned with running into dangers on their first day of travel and decide that they will be traveling _Boldly_. The GM lays out the stakes, which are fairly standard:
* An automatic _Speed_ just for traveling.
* 2 _Affliction_ (_Fatigue_) _Dangers_ for each player; one for their character, and one for their spirit.
* An _Indiscretion_ _Danger_ for each player.
